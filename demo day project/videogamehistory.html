History of Video Games

Here's the history of video games in a nutshell. Be aware that I won't cover the specifics of each event.

  • Video games had their start in research laboratories
  • 1952: British professor A.S. Douglas created OXO
  • 1958: William Higinbotham created Tennis for Two on a large analog computer and connected oscilloscope screen for the annual visitorâ€™s day at the Brookhaven National Laboratory in Upton, New York
  • 1962: Steve Russell at MIT invented Spacewar!, a computer-based space combat video game for the PDP-1, then a cutting-edge computer found in most universities
  • 1967: Developers at Sanders Associates, Inc., led by Ralph Baer, invented a prototype multiplayer, multi-program video game console that can be played on a television; they dubbed it "The Brown Box"
  • 1972: Sanders then liscensed their product to Magnavox, who released the device as the Magnavox Odyssey. On the same year, Atari created Pong, the first arcade video game that was inspired by one of the games for the Odyssey
  • 1977: Atari released the Atari 2600; it's a home console that features joysticks and interchangeable game cartriges that played multi-colored games
  • 1983: North American video game marker experienced a major "crash" due to a plethora of factors, including an oversaturated video game console market and a surplus of over-hyped, low-quality games (e.g. E.T. for the Atari 2600). The crash led to the bankrupcy of many video game companies. On the same year, Nintendo released the Family Computer in Japan
  • 1985: With the release of the Nintendo Entertainment System in North America (with its improved 8-bit graphics, colors, sounds, and gameplay), the video game industry starts to recover after the crash
