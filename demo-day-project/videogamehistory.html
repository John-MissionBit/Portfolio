History of Video Games (in brief)

Here's the history of video games in a nutshell. Be aware that I won't cover the specifics of each event.

    Early History of Video Games

  • Video games had their start in research laboratories
  • 1952: British professor A.S. Douglas created OXO
  • 1958: William Higinbotham created Tennis for Two on a large analog computer and connected oscilloscope screen for the annual visitorâ€™s day at the Brookhaven National Laboratory in Upton, New York
  • 1962: Steve Russell at MIT invented Spacewar!, a computer-based space combat video game for the PDP-1, then a cutting-edge computer found in most universities
  • 1967: Developers at Sanders Associates, Inc., led by Ralph Baer, invented a prototype multiplayer, multi-program video game console that can be played on a television; they dubbed it "The Brown Box"

    • First Generation of Video Games

  • 1972: Sanders then liscensed their product to Magnavox, who released the device as the Magnavox Odyssey. On the same year, Atari created Pong, the first arcade video game that was inspired by one of the games for the Odyssey

    • Second Generation

  • 1977: Atari released the Atari 2600; it's a home console that features joysticks and interchangeable game cartriges that played multi-colored games
  • 1983: North American video game marker experienced a major "crash" due to a plethora of factors, including an oversaturated video game console market and a surplus of over-hyped, low-quality games (e.g. E.T. for the Atari 2600). The crash led to the bankrupcy of many video game companies. On the same year, Nintendo released the Family Computer in Japan; and that eventually leads to...

    • The Third Generation

  • 1985: With the release of the Nintendo Entertainment System in North America (with its improved 8-bit graphics, colors, sounds, and gameplay), the video game industry starts to recover after the crash
  • 1989: Nintendo popularized handheld gaming with the release of the 8-bit Game Boy. Sega released the 16-bit Genesis as a successor to their previous console, the 8-bit Master System.

    • Fourth Generation

  • 1991: Nintendo released the Super Nintendo Entertainmet System to compete with Sega, thus initiating the first Console War

    • Fifth Generation

  • 1995: Sega released the Saturn in North America, which was the first 32-bit console that played games on CDs rather than cartridges. It sold $100 more than the Playstation that was released later that year
  • 1996: Nintendo released their 64-bit console called the Nintendo 64, which still uses cartridges instead of CDs. Sony's stroing third-party support helped the Playstation secure numerous exclusive titles

    • Sixth Generation

  • 2000: The Playstation 2 was released and was the first console to use DVDs; it also can play PS1 games as well. It went up against the Sega Dreamcast, the Nintendo Gamecube, and Microsoct's Xbox. It has become the best-selling video game console of all time.
  • 2001: The Dreamcast was a commercial failure that ended Sega's console effort, becoming a third-party software company after it was discontinued in 2001

    • Seventh Generation

  • 2005: Xbox 360 was released, and was lauded for its online gaming ecosystem
  • 2006: The Playstation 3 and Wii was released. The Playstation 3 can play Blu-rays, but now is facing stiff competition. The Wii ultimately sold well due to its active motion controls, and appealing to a large amount of the general public

    • Eighth Generation (current generation)

  • 2012: Nintendo released the Wii U, which ended up being a commercial failure; it was discontinued on 2017
  • 2013: Sony and Microsoft released the Playstation 4 and Xbox One, respectively.
  • 2016: Sony released a more powerful version of its console, called the Playstation 4 Pro; it's the first console capable of 4K video output
  • 2017: Nintendo released the Switch, the only gaming system that allows both television-based and handheld gaming. Microsoft released the Xbox One X later that year; it will also play in 4K

Sources: History.com, Smithsonian Institution
